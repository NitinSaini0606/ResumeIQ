# Microsoft – Hiring Process for Tech Roles

## Company Overview
Microsoft Corporation is an American multinational technology company headquartered in Redmond, Washington.
Under CEO Satya Nadella, Microsoft transformed from a competitive "know-it-all" culture to a collaborative "learn-it-all" philosophy — and this shows directly in their interviews.

- **Founded:** 1975
- **Headquarters:** Redmond, Washington, USA
- **CEO:** Satya Nadella
- **India Offices:** Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Noida, Gurugram
- **Products:** Windows, Azure, Office 365, GitHub, LinkedIn, Xbox, Teams, Copilot, Bing
- **Acceptance Rate:** Approximately 1–2% from online application to hire for internship and new grad roles. Cold application to phone screen probability is about 10%.
- **Interview Difficulty:** Rated 3.12 out of 5 on Glassdoor — considered less adversarial than Google or Meta. 66.3% of candidates rate their experience as positive.

## Microsoft Engineering Levels

Microsoft uses a numeric leveling system for engineers:

- **Level 59** – SDE I (New Grad, 0–1 year)
- **Level 60** – SDE I (1–2 years experience)
- **Level 61** – SDE II (2–5 years)
- **Level 62** – SDE II (4–6 years)
- **Level 63** – Senior SDE (6–10 years) — this is the "career level", no promotion pressure here
- **Level 64** – Senior SDE II
- **Level 65** – Principal SDE
- **Level 67+** – Partner / Distinguished Engineer
- **Level 70+** – VP and above (very rare)

Most engineers reach Level 63 in 3–5 years, though some take 6+ years.

## Hiring Tracks

### 1. University / New Grad Hiring (Level 59–60)
For final year students or recent graduates.
On-campus recruitment or direct application on careers.microsoft.com.
Focus is on DSA coding rounds and behavioral questions.
System design is rarely asked at this level.

### 2. Experienced / Lateral Hiring (Level 61 and above)
For candidates with 2+ years of industry experience.
System Design and Low Level Design (LLD) rounds are added.
More senior roles (Level 63+) include deeper system design and architecture discussions.

### 3. Internship Programs
Multiple internship tracks available — see section below.
Process: OA + 2–3 technical rounds for most internship roles.
Average hiring time: 29 days from application to offer.

## Internship Programs at Microsoft (2025–2026)

### Explore Microsoft Program
Specifically designed for first and second year college students.
Duration: 8 weeks in India, 12 weeks in the USA.
Students work in a collaborative pod alongside other Explore interns.
Experience both Software Engineering and Program Manager disciplines.
Eligibility: First or second year undergraduate students in CS, CE, IT, Data Science, EE, or related fields.
Must have at least one additional academic year remaining after internship.
This is one of the most competitive programs — only about 300 Explorer spots globally.

### University Internship Program
For students further along in their academic careers — typically pre-final year.
Duration: 12 weeks during summer.
Eligibility: Bachelor's, Master's, MBA, or PhD students with at least one term remaining after internship.
Work on real impactful projects in Software Engineering, Data Science, Business, Design, and more.
Available globally including India (Hyderabad, Bengaluru) and USA.

### Microsoft Aspire Experience
For every new hire coming directly from undergraduate, Master's, or PhD programs.
A 2-year experience focused on talent development and learning.
Not a separate internship but the onboarding program for new grads who join full time.

### Imagine Cup — Hackathon to Internship
Microsoft's global student competition where students build innovative software solutions.
Successful participants can receive internship offers at Microsoft.
Not a traditional internship path but a recognized route for top student innovators.

### How to Apply for Internships
Visit: careers.microsoft.com/students
Filter by location, role, and program type.
Applications are basically only a resume upload — takes 5–10 minutes.
If you have a Microsoft employee referral, have them send your resume and desired role to the recruiter directly — this can massively expedite the process.
Recruiters also actively search LinkedIn — keep your profile complete and updated.
Apply early — Explorer and PM spots fill up very fast. Complete interviews no later than first week of November for summer roles.

## Step-by-Step Hiring Process (2025–2026 Updated)

### Step 1 – Application
Apply via careers.microsoft.com or through a referral from a Microsoft employee.
Referrals expedite the process significantly.
Resume tips: highlight technical projects with measurable impact, relevant coursework, internships, hackathons, and open source contributions.
Recruiters also reach out via LinkedIn InMail — keep your profile updated.

### Step 2 – Recruiter Call
Short call (30 minutes) where the recruiter discusses your background, skills, and interests.
They will align you to the right team and functional role.
Sometimes the first contact is directly an OA invite — skipping the recruiter call.
The recruiter is your biggest ally — build a good relationship and keep communication clear and to the point.

### Step 3 – Online Assessment (Codility Test)
Microsoft uses Codility for their online coding assessment.
Format: multiple algorithmic problems, easy to medium difficulty, time-limited.
Some candidates sign up for Codility beforehand to see question patterns — recommended.
Tip from a real candidate (Bhavya Dhiman, 2025): the difficulty was easy to medium — practice string and array problems on Codility before the test.
Not all roles include an OA — some go straight to phone screen.

### Step 4 – Technical Phone Screen
One-on-one interview over Microsoft Teams (45–60 minutes).
Mix of technical DSA questions and behavioral questions.
Coding is done in a plain text editor — no syntax highlighting, no autocomplete.
DSA difficulty: Easy to Medium LeetCode level.
System design is very unlikely to be asked in the phone screen.
Interviewers are not given a strict rubric — they have freedom to ask whatever they want.

### Step 5 – The Interview Loop (Onsite / Virtual — Main Round)
This is the core of Microsoft's hiring process. Typically 4–5 rounds (sometimes up to 6–7 for senior roles), each 45–60 minutes.
Conducted virtually on Microsoft Teams (previously done in-person at Microsoft offices).
Process is team-dependent — different teams run slightly different loops.

#### Round 1 and 2 – Coding / DSA Rounds
LeetCode-style problems focusing on data structures and algorithms.
Difficulty: Medium to Hard.
Topics: Arrays, Trees, Graphs, Linked Lists, Dynamic Programming, Strings.
Code on CoderPad or similar plain text tool — no IDE features.
Interviewers want to hear your thought process, not just see correct code.
After each round, interviewers can flag topics for the next interviewer to probe further.
Preferred languages: C#, Java, Python — use these if possible.

#### Round 3 – Low Level Design (LLD) Round
This is very important at Microsoft — especially for mid-level and senior roles.
Design classes, interfaces, and object-oriented systems for a given problem.
Real question (SDE2, 2024): Design a transaction filtering system with thread-safe implementation — solved using interfaces, classes, mutex, and locks in C++.
Real question (Senior, 2025): Design a JSON-driven decision tree system where the frontend runs the full flow without backend calls.
Interviewers want clean, modular, production-quality code with good abstractions.

#### Round 4 – System Design Round (HLD — Level 61 and above)
High Level Design for mid-level and senior roles.
Design scalable distributed systems similar to Microsoft's real products.
Common questions: Design a URL shortener, Design a chat system, Design a content delivery system, Design a notification service, Design a call center routing system.
For senior roles (Level 63+): questions get complex — multi-region architectures, fault-tolerant storage, traffic management at scale.
Microsoft has a unique focus on compliance-related topics in system design — data privacy, EU data processing regulations, data pipelines.
Real question (SDE2 VP round): "Microsoft wanted to open a data center — how many physical devices would be required?" — focused on non-technical thinking (electricity, area, infrastructure) not just tech.

#### Round 5 – Behavioral Round
All rounds include behavioral questions but this round is dedicated to them.
Microsoft uses the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result).
Core competencies evaluated: Growth Mindset, Collaboration, Customer Focus, Ownership, Learning from Failure.
Key Microsoft value: Be a "learn-it-all" not a "know-it-all" — this is directly from Satya Nadella's culture shift.
Common questions: Tell me about a time you failed. When did you disagree with a teammate? How do you adapt to unexpected changes? What's your favorite Microsoft product and why?
Tip: Tell real stories — conflicts with managers, deadline pressure, design disagreements, mistakes you made. Authentic ownership beats perfect polished stories.
Some candidates who performed well technically were still rejected due to poor behavioral responses.

#### Round 6 – AA Round (As Appropriate) — Senior Roles Only
The AA (As Appropriate) round is a final interview with a senior Microsoft executive or VP.
Only happens if earlier rounds go well — getting invited is a strong signal you are likely to get an offer.
The AA interviewer can override negative feedback from earlier rounds OR confirm a hire decision.
Focus: project deep-dives — "Why did you make that technical decision?" and "How did you measure impact?"
If the interviewer starts talking about team opportunities and what you could work on — that is a great sign they are selling Microsoft to you.
Real experience (Abhishek Kumar, SDE2): His AA round was with a VP at Level 70 who asked a non-technical estimation question about data center devices — not DSA at all.

### Step 6 – Hiring Committee Review
After your loop, each interviewer files notes and makes a hiring recommendation: "Strong hire", "Hire", "No hire", "Strong no hire."
Feedback forms are accessible to all interviewers in your loop — they can see each other's notes.
A cross-team hiring committee reviews your full loop and makes a final decision.
The committee also calibrates your level (typically L60 to L64) and determines compensation.

### Step 7 – Team Matching and Offer
After HC approval, you are matched with a team.
You can indicate team preferences to the recruiter — but this may or may not have impact.
Most people end up on teams related to the teams their interviewers are on.
Offer includes: Base Salary + Signing Bonus + Annual Performance Bonus + RSUs.
Compensation is negotiable — especially if you have competing offers. Use Levels.fyi data to anchor your negotiation.

## System Design and LLD Topics (Level 61 and Above)

### Low Level Design (LLD) — Very Important at Microsoft
- Object-oriented design principles — SOLID, design patterns
- Class design, interface design, inheritance
- Thread safety — mutex, locks, concurrent data structures
- Transaction and filtering systems
- API design with clean abstractions

### High Level Design (HLD) — System Design
- Designing scalable distributed systems
- Load balancing and horizontal scaling
- Caching strategies (Redis, CDN)
- SQL vs NoSQL trade-offs
- Message queues (Kafka, Azure Service Bus)
- Microservices and API design
- Rate limiting and throttling
- Compliance-aware design — EU data processing, GDPR, data privacy
- Fault tolerance and high availability

### Common System Design Questions at Microsoft
- Design a URL shortener
- Design a chat system like Microsoft Teams
- Design a call center routing system
- Design a content delivery network
- Design a notification system
- Design a transaction filtering system
- Design a decision tree system
- Design a distributed cache
- Design Azure Blob Storage or similar file storage

## Behavioral Preparation — Growth Mindset is Everything

Microsoft's culture under Satya Nadella is built on the "learn-it-all" philosophy. Every behavioral round evaluates whether you embody this.

Core competencies evaluated:
- **Growth Mindset:** Ability to learn through mistakes, take risks, value teamwork over competition
- **Collaboration:** Working across teams, respecting diverse perspectives
- **Customer Focus:** Tying technical decisions back to user and customer impact
- **Ownership:** Taking accountability, not blaming others
- **Learning from Failure:** Honest reflection on mistakes and what you changed

### Common Behavioral Questions
- Tell me about a time you failed and what you learned.
- When did you disagree with your manager or teammate? What happened?
- Describe a time you had to adapt to an unexpected change.
- Tell me about a project you built from scratch — what were the challenges?
- What is your favorite Microsoft product and why?
- Tell me about a time you mentored someone.
- How do you handle situations where requirements are unclear?

Prepare 6–8 STAR stories. Be authentic — do not try to present perfect stories. Microsoft interviewers can tell when someone is being genuine versus rehearsed.

## Tips to Crack Microsoft (2025 Updated)

- Practice 100–150 LeetCode problems — focus on Trees, Graphs, and Arrays first.
- Practice coding without IDE autocomplete — Microsoft uses bare-bones text editors.
- Think out loud — Microsoft interviewers want to hear your reasoning, not just see code.
- Prepare LLD (Low Level Design) seriously — it is often the deciding round at Microsoft.
- Use STAR format for behavioral answers — prepare 6–8 real authentic stories.
- Be a "learn-it-all" not a "know-it-all" — this is the single most important cultural signal.
- Prepare genuine answers for "What is your favorite Microsoft product?" — they really ask this.
- Show growth mindset — talk about failures and what you actually learned and changed.
- Practice system design by reading the Azure Engineering Blog — shows you understand Microsoft's real engineering challenges.
- Apply early for internships — Explorer and PM spots fill up extremely fast.
- Get a referral if possible — it significantly expedites the process.
- Do mock interview loops — block half-days and simulate the full loop with camera on, plain text editor, timer running.
- For senior roles, prepare estimation questions and non-technical thinking problems — the AA round can surprise you.
- Compensation is negotiable — use Levels.fyi data to anchor negotiations, especially if you have competing offers.

Riya was a 3rd year IT student at IGDTUW who was never a hardcore DSA fan.
The hiring process was on-campus: Online Assessment (easy to medium LeetCode) followed by 2 technical rounds of 45 minutes each.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does Microsoft hire from non-tier-1 colleges in India?**
A: Yes. Microsoft hires from all colleges including non-IIT and non-NIT institutions. Skills, projects, and relevant experience matter more than college brand for experienced hires. For campus hiring, they recruit from many universities globally.

**Q: Is system design asked for new grads at Microsoft?**
A: Generally no. Level 59 and 60 candidates rarely get system design questions. The focus is on DSA coding and behavioral. If system design appears it is at a much lower bar.

**Q: What is the AA round?**
A: The As Appropriate round is a final interview with a senior Microsoft executive. It only happens if earlier rounds go well — being invited is a strong positive signal. The AA interviewer can override negative earlier feedback or confirm a hire decision.

**Q: Is LLD (Low Level Design) asked at Microsoft?**
A: Yes — and it is often the most important differentiating round for SDE2 and above. Practice object-oriented design, class design, interface design, and thread-safe implementations.

**Q: Can I reapply after rejection at Microsoft?**
A: Yes. Microsoft allows reapplication after 6–12 months. Use that time to strengthen the areas where you struggled.

**Q: What programming languages does Microsoft prefer?**
A: C#, Java, and Python are the most common. Microsoft teams mostly use these. Use one of these in your interview if possible.

**Q: Does Microsoft support remote work?**
A: Yes. Microsoft supports hybrid and remote models depending on the team and location. Check the specific job listing for work model details.

**Q: How important is the behavioral round at Microsoft?**
A: Very important. Some candidates who did well technically still got rejected for behavioral reasons. The growth mindset and learn-it-all philosophy are non-negotiable at Microsoft under Satya Nadella.

**Q: What is the Explore Microsoft Program?**
A: A summer internship specifically for first and second year college students. 8 weeks in India, 12 weeks in the USA. Students work in pods experiencing both Software Engineering and Program Manager roles. Only about 300 Explorer spots exist globally — apply early.
